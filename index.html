What the Data Shows

Across 90+ universities, admission selectivity and low-income enrollment move in opposite directions. Schools admitting fewer than 10% of applicants enroll just 2–5% of students from the bottom income quintile — while schools admitting 60–90% enroll 8–15%.

The financial aid picture is more nuanced. Many highly selective private universities charge families earning under $30k almost nothing: Princeton charges $41/year, MIT and Stanford near zero. That looks like radical generosity — but these schools admit so few low-income applicants that the policy barely moves enrollment numbers.

Meanwhile, less selective public universities like UC Riverside (76% admit rate) and UC Irvine (29%) enroll far more low-income students as a raw share of enrollment. In practice, access comes more from open admissions than from need-blind aid at elite schools.

The core argument: top universities talk about meritocracy and need-blind aid, but the data shows a consistent pattern where admission selectivity predicts lower low-income enrollment — regardless of financial aid generosity.

Data: College Scorecard (U.S. Dept. of Education) + Opportunity Insights Mobility Report Cards. Income quintile shares reflect ~2013–2017 cohorts.
